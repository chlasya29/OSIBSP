01

The Man Behind the Brush

Born into the aristocratic Kilimanoor family in 1848, Ravi Varma grew up in a palace coloured with murals, manuscripts and music. By eighteen, he had taught himself oil painting — a medium then alien to India — and would soon redefine how a nation looked at itself.

He fused the academic realism of European masters with the poetry of Indian mythology. Sita stepped out of the Ramayana, Shakuntala out of Kalidasa, Damayanti out of legend — each rendered with skin, breath, and longing.

In 1894, he founded the Ravi Varma Press near Bombay, mass-producing colour oleographs of gods and goddesses. For the first time, divinity travelled by post — finding shrines in mud huts, palaces and railway stations alike.

03

A Life in Brushstrokes

  1. 1848

    Born at Kilimanoor

    Arrives in a palace of poets and painters, on the 29th of April.

  2. 1862

    The Royal Studio

    Taken to Travancore palace; studies under Rama Swamy Naidu and Dutch artist Theodore Jensen.

  3. 1873

    First gold medal

    Wins the Governor's award at Madras for Nair Lady Adorning Her Hair.

  4. 1881

    Gaekwad's commission

    Maharaja of Baroda commissions fourteen mythological paintings — the works that immortalise him.

  5. 1894

    Ravi Varma Press

    Sets up India's first lithographic press at Ghatkopar, Bombay. Gods finally reach the masses.

  6. 1904

    Kaisar-i-Hind

    Awarded by Viceroy Lord Curzon — the first Indian artist to receive the honour.

  7. 1906

    The final canvas

    Passes away on 2 October at Attingal, leaving behind a nation that learned to see itself.
